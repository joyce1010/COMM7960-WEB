Traditional Food

1 Icelandic Lamb

Icelandic lamb is prized for its quality and distinctive flavor, which is attributed to the lambs' free-range lifestyle, grazing on unpolluted highland pastures filled with wild herbs and berries. The meat is tender and mildly gamey, often prepared roasted with a crust of local herbs or slow-cooked in stews. The traditional Icelandic lamb soup, known as "Kjötsúpa," is a popular dish that combines lamb with root vegetables and herbs in a rich broth.

2 Hákarl

This comforting stew is a staple in Icelandic cuisine, featuring a mix of boiled fish (commonly cod, haddock, or halibut), potatoes, onions, and plenty of béchamel sauce for creaminess. It's a simple yet hearty dish, often seasoned with salt, pepper, and sometimes curry powder to enhance the flavor. Plokkfiskur is typically served with dark rye bread and butter, making it a satisfying meal especially during Iceland's colder months.

3 Bread Bowl Soup*

**Bread Bowl Soup** in Iceland is a cozy dish where hearty soup is served in a hollowed-out loaf of bread, combining the meal and dish into one. Commonly filled with seafood or lamb soup, the bread soaks up the flavor, offering a delicious, edible bowl experience.

4 Skyr

Similar to yogurt, but technically a soft cheese, Skyr has been part of Icelandic cuisine for over a thousand years. It's made from pasteurized skim milk and a unique set of bacteria cultures that give it a thick, creamy texture and a slightly sour taste. High in protein and calcium but low in fat, Skyr is often served at breakfast topped with fresh berries, honey, or granola. It's also used in a variety of Icelandic desserts and sauces.
